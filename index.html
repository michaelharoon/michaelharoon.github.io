Tutoring: a Lesson for Everyone

As a tutor at Long Beach City College, I realized that students struggled not because they didn't understand the content, but because they did not know how to learn. The process of learning should be simple, but it depends on the person in question. A commonality, though, is that people do not ask why they are struggling. When they do find the reason why they are struggling, they then might not know how to solve the problem, or they found how to solve the problem but they do not know how to implement their solution.

Let me regress. Firstly, it should be known that all problems have solutions -- maybe not pretty or simple solutions, but solutions nonetheless. Think of the process of solving a problem as a graph.

The Problem Graph

The graph above shows an ideal scenario. This scenario usually represents the general approach to solving simple problems, and it might occur many times within an hour. Nevertheless, notice the process. This "mind" identifies the cause, then figures out a solution (or many solutions) to solve the problem. Lastly, this mind implements its solution(s). Some solutions might include altering the cause or simply removing it entirely. A real example of this diagram is how my roommate recently reduced his stress during pressured times. He was overwhelmed, nearly burnt out, and borderline depressed. Instead of succumbing to the stress, he took a break from work and gave himself time to think. He wondered why he was overwhelmed, burntout, and depressed. He knew it was school that was the cause, but what of school?--: It was his need to pass classes and exams with high marks so that he can be competetive for law school admissions. He contimplated how to do so. The first solution was obvious: try hard and do well, which he started doing. But he is not in the position where simply putting in time will help, so he needed another solution. He thought that if his grades and scores were not competetive enough, then strong letters of recommendation were required. He then asked for letters from people he thought would write strongly about him. Finally, he felt relieved that there existed a solution to something that felt hopeless.

My roommate had a problem, figured the underlying cause, found possible solutions, and implemented them. He was no longer depressivley stressed. However, solving his main problem led to more problems. This is a given in the life we live. So, the goal is to not just solve given problems and become relieved, the goal is to learn how to solve many problems without burning out. The key to this is grit.
